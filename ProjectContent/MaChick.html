MaChick
May 2018

Overview

Core Gameplay

Everything MaChick has to offer is shown in the video above. There was a lot more planned though.

The core idea is, that the magical chicken called MaChick has three types of attacks: Forwards, Inwards and Backwards (lay egg).
She can also control three elements: Fire, Water, Earth.
By combining directions and elements she gets 9 different abilities, which can all interact with each other.
If MaChick lays a Fire Egg for example, she can make it explode by shooting fire or a rock against it. If she spews water on it instead, she can extinguish it safely.


Why I'm proud of MaChick

MaChick is the second project I ever made. A lot of planning went into it, but just a tiny bit of it was actually realized.

It was not a failure at all though, because I learned a lot while working on it.
To make various interactions between abilities and objects possible, I learned how to use interfaces.
A friend made custom animations and I implemented them.
The controls for MaChick feel smooth and interesting.

Even the hit registering for the fire attack is custom made. The white boxes shown in the video are usually invisible and are used as triggers. If a box intersects with an object which implements my IBurnable-interface, the function 'Burn' is called. What this function does, is totally up to the object which was hit.


Why MaChick got shelved

MaChick was a way too ambitious project. Creating a minimum viable product would have been the best course of action, but instead I focused on story and world-building.

I believe the scope of the game was the reason why multiple people, who had expressed their intention to work on MaChick, lost interest.
After the first prototype was done, I wasn't satisfied with the core gameplay. My biggest doubt was the enemies' behaviour which was way too linear. Back then, I didn't feel experienced enough to create something I would be satisfied with.

I decided, that I'd create only simple prototypes until I'd feel confident enough to try something more challenging.
Of course I was disappointed at first, but in hindsight, I am happy that I worked on MaChick and 'failed' with it.
Testing my limits this early helped me understand, how good I was, and what I wanted to learn.
